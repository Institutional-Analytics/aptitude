How Aptitude Testing Can Help

The more information you have, the better your decision will be. Guidance counselors can help you appraise your interests, academic achievements, and personality characteristics. Your parents can give you the benefit of their experience. But most importantly, knowing yourself — knowing your own strengths — is essential.

What Are Aptitudes?

Aptitudes are natural talents — special abilities for doing, or learning to do, certain kinds of things easily and quickly. They have little to do with knowledge or culture, or education, or even interests. They have to do with who you are and how you were created.

You are most likely to enjoy and find satisfying work which uses your aptitudes. This test is a tool for self-discovery. Please answer honestly based on your natural preferences and instincts. There are no time limits.

The test has 4 parts: personal information; work-style & personality (10 questions); core academic inclinations (10 questions); and interest & scenario-based questions (20 questions). Together these are scored against university majors, each out of 50 points. A separate subject placement quiz (Part 4) then measures current knowledge to refine the recommendation. Allow approximately 60–90 minutes, and complete the test in one sitting — your answers are not saved if you close or reload this page.

Three University Majors You Are Considering

Part 1: Work-Style and Personality

This section measures behavioral tendencies, motivations, and preferences. There are no right or wrong answers — it's about fit. Choose the option that feels most like you.

Part 2: Core Academic Inclinations

These questions explore your intellectual interests, preferred modes of learning, and the subjects you naturally gravitate toward.

Part 3: Interest and Scenario-Based Questions

What would you do in each situation? Pick the option most natural to you.

Part 4: Subject Placement Quiz

This section has graded questions across six subjects. Answer each to the best of your ability — there is no penalty for guessing. You may skip a subject if you have no background in it, but answering provides a more accurate report. These scores measure current knowledge and are used to refine your major recommendation.
